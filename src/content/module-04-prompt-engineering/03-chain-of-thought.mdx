# Chain of Thought Prompting

Chain of Thought (CoT) prompting encourages Claude to reason through problems step by step before giving a final answer. This dramatically improves accuracy on complex reasoning, math, logic, and multi-step tasks.

## Why It Works

When Claude "thinks out loud," it:
- Catches mistakes in its own reasoning
- Breaks complex problems into manageable steps
- Produces more accurate, verifiable answers
- Makes its reasoning transparent and auditable

## Basic Chain of Thought

Simply ask Claude to show its work:

```
What is 17% of 342? Show your step-by-step calculation.
```

Or more generally:

```
Think through this problem step by step before giving your answer.
```

## The `<thinking>` Tag Pattern

A powerful technique is to ask Claude to separate reasoning from the final answer:

```
Analyze this contract clause. 
First, write your analysis inside <thinking> tags.
Then provide your final recommendation outside the tags.

Clause: "Licensor may terminate this agreement with 30 days notice 
if Licensee fails to maintain a gross revenue of $10,000/month for 
any two consecutive months."
```

Claude's output:
```
<thinking>
This is a revenue-based termination clause. Let me analyze the key elements:
1. Termination trigger: gross revenue below $10,000/month
2. Duration requirement: two consecutive months (not just one)
3. Notice period: 30 days — licensor must give advance warning
4. Potential risks for the licensee...
</thinking>

This clause creates significant risk for the licensee. The revenue threshold of $10,000/month could be challenging for early-stage businesses...
```

## Zero-Shot vs Few-Shot CoT

### Zero-Shot CoT
Just add "Let's think step by step":
```
If a train travels 120 miles in 2 hours, then slows down and covers 
90 miles in 3 hours, what is its average speed for the whole journey?
Let's think step by step.
```

### Few-Shot CoT
Provide examples of the reasoning pattern:
```
Q: There are 5 birds and 3 cats. How many legs total?
A: Birds have 2 legs each: 5 × 2 = 10. Cats have 4 legs each: 3 × 4 = 12. Total: 10 + 12 = 22 legs.

Q: There are 4 spiders and 2 dogs. How many legs total?
A: [Claude follows the same pattern]
```

## When to Use Chain of Thought

| Use CoT | Skip CoT |
|---|---|
| Math problems | Simple factual lookups |
| Logic puzzles | Short creative tasks |
| Multi-step decisions | Straightforward summaries |
| Code debugging | Casual conversation |
| Legal/medical analysis | Quick classifications |

## Extended Thinking

Claude models also support a formal **Extended Thinking** mode (covered in Module 6) which gives Claude a dedicated reasoning space with higher token budgets for the most complex tasks.

## Key Takeaways

- Chain of Thought prompting improves accuracy on complex reasoning tasks
- Use "think step by step" or `<thinking>` tags to trigger reasoning
- Few-shot CoT is powerful when you can provide reasoning examples
